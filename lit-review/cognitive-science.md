# Literature Review: Cognitive Science of Note-Taking During Reading

---

## 1. NOTE-TAKING AND READING COMPREHENSION

### 1.1 The Encoding vs. External Storage Framework

**Di Vesta, F. J., & Gray, G. S. (1972). Listening and note taking. *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 64(3), 321-325.**
- **Claims**: Established the dual-function framework: (1) *encoding* -- the act of writing deepens processing and imprints into long-term memory; (2) *external storage* -- the written record enables later review. Found support for both functions; note-takers recalled more even without reviewing notes.
- **Methods**: Experimental, lecture-based.
- **Relevance**: Foundational framework. Any system capturing reading context + notes should support both encoding (the active annotation process) and storage (retrievable, reviewable records linked to source).

**Kobayashi, K. (2005). What limits the encoding effect of note-taking? A meta-analytic examination. *Contemporary Educational Psychology*, 30(2), 242-262.**
- **Claims**: Meta-analysis of 57 studies. Small to modest positive encoding effects. Visual presentation interfered with note-taking process; longer presentations did not help. Recall tests detected the encoding effect more than recognition or higher-order tests.
- **Methods**: Meta-analysis. Moderator analysis on 7 variables.
- **Relevance**: The encoding effect is real but fragile -- moderated by presentation mode, test type, and task demands. System design should reduce extraneous load during the encoding act.

**Kobayashi, K. (2006). Combined effects of note-taking/-reviewing on learning and the enhancement through interventions: A meta-analytic review. *Educational Psychology*, 26(3), 459-477.**
- **Claims**: Modest additive effects for note-taking + review. Instruction on note-taking strategy enhanced effects compared to no-instruction note-taking.
- **Methods**: Meta-analysis.
- **Relevance**: Notes are most valuable when they can be reviewed later -- storage function adds to encoding. System should optimize both capture AND retrieval.

### 1.2 Mueller & Oppenheimer and the Replication Debate

**Mueller, P. A., & Oppenheimer, D. M. (2014). The pen is mightier than the keyboard: Advantages of longhand over laptop note taking. *Psychological Science*, 25(6), 1159-1168.**
- **Claims**: Three experiments showed laptop note-takers performed worse on conceptual questions. Proposed mechanism: laptop users transcribe verbatim rather than processing and reframing, which is detrimental to learning.
- **Methods**: 3 lab experiments; participants watched TED talks and took notes longhand or laptop. Tested factual and conceptual recall.
- **Sample**: Study 1: N=67; Study 2: N=151; Study 3: N=109.
- **Relevance**: Seminal paper on modality effects. The key insight is not pen vs. keyboard per se, but *verbatim transcription vs. generative processing*. A note-taking system should encourage paraphrasing/summarizing, not copying.

**Morehead, K., Dunlosky, J., & Rawson, K. A. (2019). How much mightier is the pen than the keyboard for note-taking? A replication and extension of Mueller and Oppenheimer (2014). *Educational Psychology Review*, 31(3), 753-780.**
- **Claims**: Direct replication + extension. Could NOT reproduce the original conceptual-question advantage for longhand. Added eWriter and no-notes conditions. Some trends favored longhand but none were statistically significant. Mini meta-analysis of replications showed small, nonsignificant effects.
- **Methods**: Direct replication with same materials; extended with eWriter condition.
- **Sample**: N=142.
- **Relevance**: The medium (pen vs. keyboard) may matter less than the *cognitive strategy* employed. System design should focus on promoting generative processing regardless of input modality.

**Urry, H. L., et al. (2021). Don't ditch the laptop just yet: A direct replication of Mueller and Oppenheimer's (2014) Study 1 plus mini meta-analyses across similar studies. *Psychological Science*, 32(3), 326-339.**
- **Claims**: Another failed replication. Mini meta-analyses across similar studies showed small, non-significant effects.
- **Methods**: Direct replication, registered report format.
- **Relevance**: Further evidence that the pen-vs-keyboard framing is oversimplified. The action of note-taking matters more than the tool.

### 1.3 Integrative Reviews

**Jansen, R. S., Lakens, D., & IJsselsteijn, W. A. (2017). An integrative review of the cognitive costs and benefits of note-taking. *Educational Research Review*, 22, 223-233.**
- **Claims**: Part 1 synthesizes four research lines on encoding: (1) manipulation of lecture material, (2) manipulation of note-taking method, (3) individual differences, (4) testing procedures. Part 2 identifies five forms of cognitive load induced by note-taking. Argues CLT provides the best integrative framework. Notes the fragmented nature of the literature.
- **Methods**: Integrative literature review.
- **Relevance**: Critical framing paper. Cognitive load theory as unifying lens for note-taking research. A system that reduces extraneous cognitive load during annotation (e.g., by keeping notes anchored to source context) should amplify encoding benefits.

**Boch, F., & Piolat, A. (2005). Note taking and learning: A summary of research. *The WAC Journal*, 16, 101-113.**
- **Claims**: Note-taking intertwines comprehension and written production. Engages integration, inference, planning, and assessment. Demands more cognitive effort than reading alone but less than original composition.
- **Methods**: Literature review.
- **Relevance**: Note-taking is not just recording -- it's a complex cognitive activity combining comprehension with production. System affordances should support this dual process.

## 2. HANDWRITTEN VS. TYPED NOTES

### 2.1 Meta-Analyses

**Flanigan, A. E., Wheeler, J., Colliot, T., Lu, J., & Kiewra, K. A. (2024). Typed versus handwritten lecture notes and college student achievement: A meta-analysis. *Educational Psychology Review*, 36(3), Article 79.**
- **Claims**: 24 studies across 21 articles. Handwritten notes -> higher achievement (Hedges' g = 0.248, p < .001). Typed notes -> more volume (Hedges' g = 0.919, p < .001). Effect was larger for longer lectures and when course grades (not lab tests) were the outcome. Lab studies with controlled distractions showed smaller/no differences.
- **Methods**: Meta-analysis with moderator analyses.
- **Relevance**: The effect is small but real in ecologically valid settings. Typing produces more text but less learning. A system should prioritize quality/depth of annotation over quantity.

**Allen, A., & Mahdi, A. (2021). The effect of notetaking method on academic performance: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Contemporary Educational Psychology*, 67, 102004.**
- **Claims**: Systematic review finding that note-taking method effects are moderated by study design (lab vs. classroom), distraction availability, and outcome measure type.
- **Methods**: Systematic review and meta-analysis.
- **Relevance**: Contextual factors (distraction, ecological validity) moderate the handwriting advantage. System design should account for real-world usage conditions.

### 2.2 Neuroscience Evidence

**Van der Meer, A. L. H., & Van der Weel, F. R. (2024). Handwriting but not typewriting leads to widespread brain connectivity: A high-density EEG study with implications for the classroom. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 14, 1219945.**
- **Claims**: 36 university students. Handwriting produced far more widespread theta/alpha connectivity (3.5-12.5 Hz) in central and parietal brain regions compared to typing. These frequency bands are associated with memory formation and encoding of new information.
- **Methods**: 256-channel high-density EEG. Within-subjects comparison of handwriting vs. typewriting visually presented words.
- **Sample**: N=36 university students.
- **Caveats**: Lab-based, adult population. A published commentary notes that translation to educational settings in children is not straightforward.
- **Relevance**: Neural evidence that the motor act of handwriting engages memory-relevant networks more than typing. Supports stylus/pen-based annotation in digital systems.

**James, K. H., & Engelhardt, L. (2012). The effects of handwriting experience on functional brain development in pre-literate children. *Trends in Neuroscience and Education*, 1(1), 32-42.**
- **Claims**: fMRI study with pre-literate 5-year-olds. Left fusiform gyrus (reading circuit) was recruited during letter perception only after handwriting practice -- not after typing or tracing.
- **Methods**: fMRI, pre-literate children, between-subjects.
- **Relevance**: Handwriting creates neural pathways that support reading. Suggestive that the motoric act of writing has encoding properties beyond what typing provides.

## 3. ANNOTATION LINKING AND ANCHORED ANNOTATIONS

### 3.1 Foundational Work on Annotation as Active Reading

**Marshall, C. C. (1997). Annotation: From paper books to the digital library. *Proceedings of the Second ACM International Conference on Digital Libraries*, 131-140.**
- **Claims**: Studied annotations in university textbooks. Characterized form and function of margin annotations. Annotations serve as a key practice of active reading -- highlighting, underlining, and marginal notes are all situated in the source text.
- **Methods**: Qualitative analysis of physical textbook annotations.
- **Relevance**: Foundational HCI paper on annotation. The key insight: annotations are *spatially anchored* to source material. Digital systems should preserve this spatial relationship.

**Marshall, C. C. (1998). Toward an ecology of hypertext annotation. *Proceedings of the Ninth ACM Conference on Hypertext and Hypermedia*, 40-49.**
- **Claims**: Annotation is a key way hypertexts grow and increase in value. Characterized annotation along multiple dimensions. Explored implications for hypertext design.
- **Methods**: Long-term study of annotator community; theoretical framework.
- **Relevance**: Annotation linking is not just individual cognition -- it's a social and ecological practice. Systems should support both personal and shared annotation anchored to source.

### 3.2 Social Annotation and Anchored Discussion

**Kalir, J. H., Morales, E., Fleerackers, A., & Alperin, J. P. (2020). When I saw my peers annotating: Student perceptions of social annotation for learning in multiple courses. *Information and Learning Sciences*, 121(3/4), 207-230.**
- **Claims**: Social annotation (using Hypothesis) supports reading comprehension, close reading, textual analysis, and argumentation. Students reported that seeing peers' anchored annotations enhanced their own understanding.
- **Methods**: Case study across multiple undergraduate courses; mixed methods (surveys, annotation data).
- **Relevance**: Anchoring annotations to specific passages in shared texts transforms reading from an isolating activity into socially constructed learning. Direct evidence for the value of contextual/linked annotations.

**Morales, E., Kalir, J. H., Fleerackers, A., & Alperin, J. P. (2022). Using social annotation to construct knowledge with others: A case study across undergraduate courses. *F1000Research*, 11:235.**
- **Claims**: Social annotation supported co-construction of meaning. Annotations anchored to passages promoted deeper engagement than traditional discussion forums.
- **Methods**: Case study; analysis of annotation content and patterns.
- **Relevance**: Passage-anchored annotations promote knowledge construction beyond what detached discussion provides.

**Cui, Y., et al. (2024). Empowering active learning: A social annotation tool for improving student engagement. *British Journal of Educational Technology*, 55(3), 1079-1097.**
- **Claims**: Pre-class social annotations (anchored to readings via Perusall) had a positive impact on post-class assessment performance and student engagement.
- **Methods**: Quasi-experimental.
- **Relevance**: Anchored annotations as pre-class preparation improve subsequent learning outcomes.

**Hwang, W.-Y., Wang, C.-Y., & Sharples, M. (2007). A study of multimedia annotation of web-based materials. *Computers & Education*, 48(4), 680-699.**
- **Claims**: Anchored annotation-based discussion environments led to better conversations about a text. Annotations provoked students to reflect more critically upon the primary text, especially when encountering different viewpoints on the same passage.
- **Methods**: Experimental; compared annotation conditions.
- **Relevance**: Annotations anchored to specific text locations provoke deeper critical reflection than un-anchored discussion.

### 3.3 Digital Annotation and Reading Comprehension

**List, A., & Lin, L. (2023). Content and quantity of highlights and annotations predict learning from multiple digital texts. *Computers & Education*, 199, 104791.**
- **Claims**: Undergraduates (N=278) who highlighted and annotated while reading multiple digital texts showed learning gains. Both the *content quality* and *quantity* of annotations predicted learning outcomes. Annotations that linked concepts across texts were particularly valuable.
- **Methods**: Quasi-experimental; coded annotation content.
- **Sample**: N=278 undergraduates.
- **Relevance**: Directly relevant. Annotations that link to and across source material predict learning. System should support cross-text annotation linking.

**Zhang, X., et al. (2025). How the quality of annotation influences academic reading: An eye-tracking study. *Journal of Computer Assisted Learning*, 41, e70062.**
- **Claims**: Constructive annotations (higher-level cognitive processing) attracted more visual attention than active annotations and facilitated reading comprehension more effectively. N=47.
- **Methods**: Eye-tracking + pre/post tests; between-subjects.
- **Sample**: N=47 college students.
- **Relevance**: Annotation *quality level* matters. System should scaffold constructive (generative) annotation rather than surface-level highlighting.

## 4. GENERATIVE LEARNING THEORY AND NOTE-TAKING

### 4.1 Wittrock's Generative Learning Theory

**Wittrock, M. C. (1974). Learning as a generative process. *Educational Psychologist*, 11(2), 87-95.**
- **Claims**: Learning occurs when learners actively generate connections between new information and prior knowledge. The learner's brain does not passively record information but actively constructs meaning by generating relations.
- **Methods**: Theoretical framework.
- **Relevance**: The theoretical backbone. Note-taking during reading is effective precisely when it is *generative* -- paraphrasing, summarizing, connecting to prior knowledge -- not when it is rote copying.

**Wittrock, M. C. (1992). Generative learning processes of the brain. *Educational Psychologist*, 27(4), 531-541.**
- **Claims**: Extended the generative model. Learning is "a function of the abstract and distinctive, concrete associations which the learner generates between prior experience and stimuli." Emphasized that generation of connections is the critical mechanism.
- **Methods**: Theoretical/review.
- **Relevance**: Reinforces that the *act of generating* connections during reading -- not the act of recording -- drives encoding.

### 4.2 Applied Generative Learning Strategies

**Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2016). Eight ways to promote generative learning. *Educational Psychology Review*, 28(4), 717-741.**
- **Claims**: Identifies eight generative strategies: summarizing, mapping, drawing, imagining, self-testing, self-explaining, teaching, and enacting. Each promotes learning by forcing the learner to reorganize and integrate material with prior knowledge. Reviewed evidence for each with boundary conditions.
- **Methods**: Comprehensive review of experimental literature.
- **Relevance**: Directly applicable. A note-taking system should scaffold these generative activities -- especially summarizing, mapping, and self-explaining -- during reading. These strategies go beyond passive highlighting.

**Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2015). *Learning as a generative activity: Eight learning strategies that promote understanding*. Cambridge University Press.**
- **Claims**: Book-length treatment. For each of 8 strategies: description, exemplary research, boundary conditions, and practical recommendations.
- **Methods**: Literature synthesis.
- **Relevance**: The authoritative reference for designing generative learning supports.

**Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2023). Making sense of generative learning. *Educational Psychology Review*, 35, Article 50.**
- **Claims**: Updated review. Clarified theoretical mechanisms and boundary conditions. Discussed when generative activities can backfire (e.g., when cognitive load exceeds capacity).
- **Methods**: Review/theoretical update.
- **Relevance**: Generative activities can overload working memory if not scaffolded properly. A system must balance generative demands with support.

### 4.3 Learning Strategies Effectiveness

**Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013). Improving students' learning with effective learning techniques: Promising directions from cognitive and educational psychology. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4-58.**
- **Claims**: Reviewed 10 learning techniques. Practice testing and distributed practice rated "high utility." Summarization, highlighting, and rereading rated "low utility" as commonly implemented -- but with caveats that structured summarization can be effective.
- **Methods**: Comprehensive review across materials, learning conditions, student characteristics, and criterion tasks.
- **Relevance**: Highlighting alone is low-utility. Summarization is low-utility *as commonly practiced* but can be effective when structured. System should push users beyond highlighting toward structured summarization and self-testing.

## 5. COGNITIVE LOAD AND NOTE-TAKING

**Piolat, A., Olive, T., & Kellogg, R. T. (2005). Cognitive effort during note taking. *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, 19(3), 291-312.**
- **Claims**: Used triple-task methodology to measure cognitive effort. Note-taking demands more cognitive effort than reading or learning alone, but less than creative written composition. Working memory's central executive is heavily taxed by simultaneous comprehension + selection + recording.
- **Methods**: Triple-task paradigm (writing task + reaction time task + directed retrospection).
- **Relevance**: Note-taking during reading is cognitively expensive. System design should minimize extraneous demands (UI friction, navigation) to leave capacity for germane processing.

**Peverly, S. T., Ramaswamy, V., Brown, C., Sumowski, J., Alidoost, M., & Garner, J. (2007). What predicts skill in lecture note taking? *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 99(1), 167-180.**
- **Claims**: Transcription fluency was the only significant predictor of note quality; note quality was the only predictor of test performance. Working memory and prior knowledge did not predict note quality after accounting for transcription fluency.
- **Methods**: Correlational; cognitive battery + lecture note-taking.
- **Relevance**: Motor bottleneck matters. If transcription is slow, cognitive resources are consumed by writing mechanics rather than comprehension. Typed notes may alleviate this bottleneck but at the cost of reduced generative processing. Design tension for system.

**Peverly, S. T., & Wolf, A. D. (2013). Note-taking. In J. Hattie & E. M. Anderman (Eds.), *International guide to student achievement*. Routledge.**
- **Claims**: Handwriting speed was consistently the only significant predictor of note quality across multiple studies.
- **Methods**: Review of Peverly lab's program of research.
- **Relevance**: The transcription fluency bottleneck is robust. Systems should consider hybrid approaches (typed capture + handwritten processing).

## 6. TEMPORAL DYNAMICS AND PROGRESSION OF NOTE-TAKING

*Note: This is the thinnest area in the literature. Most note-taking research treats notes as a static product rather than examining how they evolve over time during reading.*

### 6.1 Progressive Processing

**Forte, T. (2017/2022). Progressive summarization: A practical technique for designing discoverable notes. Forte Labs / *Building a Second Brain*. Simon & Schuster.**
- **Claims**: Proposes a layered approach: Layer 0 = full source, Layer 1 = initial capture, Layer 2 = bold key points, Layer 3 = highlight of highlights, Layer 4 = executive summary. Each layer is a progressive distillation.
- **Methods**: Practitioner framework (not peer-reviewed empirical research).
- **Relevance**: Not academic evidence, but the most articulated framework for *temporal progression* in note-taking. The layered approach maps onto generative learning theory: each pass forces deeper processing. Testable hypothesis for a reading system.

### 6.2 Note Quality Over Time

**Luo, L., Kiewra, K. A., Flanigan, A. E., & Peteranetz, M. S. (2018). Laptop versus longhand note taking: Effects on lecture notes and achievement. *Instructional Science*, 46(6), 947-971.**
- **Claims**: Laptop note-takers recorded more words and more verbatim text. Note quality (not quantity) predicted achievement. Investigated how note content changes across a lecture.
- **Methods**: Experimental; content analysis of notes.
- **Relevance**: Suggests that note content shifts during a lecture -- earlier notes may be more complete, later notes more selective. System could track this progression.

## 7. EYE-TRACKING AND READING

### 7.1 Foundational Reviews

**Rayner, K. (1998). Eye movements in reading and information processing: 20 years of research. *Psychological Bulletin*, 124(3), 372-422.**
- **Claims**: Comprehensive review establishing that eye movements reflect moment-to-moment cognitive processes. Fixations average 200-250ms with high variability. Covered perceptual span, integration across saccades, eye movement control, and individual differences.
- **Methods**: Review of 20 years of eye-tracking research.
- **Relevance**: Foundational. Eye fixation data provides a window into real-time comprehension processes during reading. Could be used to identify moments of difficulty or deep processing where notes would be most valuable.

**Rayner, K. (2009). Eye movements and attention in reading, scene perception, and visual search. *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 62(8), 1457-1506.**
- **Claims**: Updated review covering developments since 1998. Eye movements are tightly linked to attention allocation. Discussed five central facts about reading eye movements that any model must account for.
- **Methods**: Review.
- **Relevance**: Eye tracking can reveal attention allocation patterns during reading that could inform when to prompt or scaffold note-taking.

### 7.2 Eye-Tracking and Comprehension

**Rayner, K., Chace, K. H., Slattery, T. J., & Ashby, J. (2006). Eye movements as reflections of comprehension processes in reading. *Scientific Studies of Reading*, 10(3), 241-255.**
- **Claims**: Fixation patterns are sensitive to both text features and reader comprehension. Longer fixations, more regressions, and shorter saccades indicate difficulty.
- **Methods**: Eye-tracking during reading; analysis of fixation metrics.
- **Relevance**: Comprehension difficulty is detectable from gaze. A system with eye-tracking could identify passages where readers struggle and prompt annotation.

**Jarodzka, H., & Brand-Gruwel, S. (2017). Tracking the reading eye: Towards a model of real-world reading. *Journal of Computer Assisted Learning*, 33(3), 193-201.**
- **Claims**: Proposed moving beyond laboratory reading paradigms toward understanding real-world reading. Discussed integration of eye-tracking with digital reading environments.
- **Methods**: Theoretical/review.
- **Relevance**: Directly relevant to building systems that combine eye-tracking with annotation during naturalistic reading.

### 7.3 Eye-Tracking Combined with Annotation

**Filetti, M., Tavakoli, H. R., Ravaja, N., & Jacucci, G. (2019). PeyeDF: An eye-tracking application for reading and self-indexing research. arXiv:1904.12152.**
- **Claims**: Built a PDF reader with integrated eye-tracking. Demonstrated that features from gaze + reading behavior data collected in the past can predict future reading comprehension. Supports annotations, tagging, and collaborative work.
- **Methods**: System paper + experiment.
- **Relevance**: Directly relevant as a precedent system. Combines eye-tracking, annotation, and comprehension prediction in a single reading environment.

**Zhang, X., et al. (2025). How the quality of annotation influences academic reading: An eye-tracking study. *Journal of Computer Assisted Learning*, 41, e70062.**
- (See Section 3.3 above for full entry.)
- **Additional relevance to eye-tracking**: Used eye-tracking to show that constructive annotations attracted more visual attention and produced different reading patterns than active annotations.

**Shubi, O., Hadar, C. A., & Berzak, Y. (2024). Decoding reading goals from eye movements. *Proceedings of ACL 2025*. arXiv:2410.20779.**
- **Claims**: Successfully distinguished information-seeking vs. comprehension reading goals from eye movement data alone. Transformer-based models with scanpath representations performed best. Accurate predictions possible in real-time before reader finishes.
- **Methods**: Large-scale eye-tracking; machine learning classification.
- **Relevance**: Reading goals are decodable from gaze. A system could infer whether a reader is scanning vs. deeply reading and adapt note-taking prompts accordingly.

**Jakobi, D. N., et al. (2024). PoTeC: A German naturalistic eye-tracking-while-reading corpus. arXiv:2403.00506.**
- **Claims**: 75 participants reading 12 scientific texts; expert vs. novice comparison in 2x2x2 design. Includes comprehension questions and background knowledge tests.
- **Methods**: Corpus; naturalistic eye-tracking.
- **Relevance**: Provides evidence that domain expertise changes reading patterns. A note-taking system could adapt to reader expertise level.

### 7.4 Multimodal Learning Analytics

**Ochoa, X. (2022). Chapter 6: Multimodal learning analytics. In *Handbook of Learning Analytics* (2nd ed.). SoLAR.**
- **Claims**: MMLA integrates diverse data sources (video, audio, physiological, digital interactions) to capture learning complexity. Data classified into digital, physical, physiological, psychometric, and environmental. Key objectives: behavioral trajectories, learning outcomes, engagement.
- **Methods**: Review/handbook chapter.
- **Relevance**: A system combining reading trace data, annotation data, and potentially gaze data is a multimodal learning analytics system. This chapter provides the analytical framework.

**Sharma, K., & Giannakos, M. (2020). Multimodal data capabilities for learning: What can multimodal data tell us about learning? *British Journal of Educational Technology*, 51(5), 1450-1484.**
- **Claims**: Integrating modalities reveals latent patterns overlooked by unimodal approaches. Eye-tracking is widely used to identify reading behaviors, cognitive load, and comprehension patterns.
- **Methods**: Review.
- **Relevance**: Combining eye-tracking with annotation trace data could reveal patterns invisible to either alone.

## 8. CROSS-CUTTING THEMES AND GAPS

### Key Tensions the Literature Surfaces

1. **Encoding depth vs. cognitive load**: Generative note-taking (paraphrasing, summarizing) produces deeper encoding, but it also consumes more working memory. If the system demands too much, it backfires (Fiorella & Mayer, 2023; Piolat et al., 2005).

2. **Transcription fluency as bottleneck**: Peverly's work shows handwriting speed is the primary predictor of note quality. This creates a tension with the Van der Meer EEG evidence showing handwriting engages more memory-relevant networks. Slow handwriters may benefit from typed capture + handwritten processing.

3. **Medium vs. strategy**: The Mueller & Oppenheimer replication failures (Morehead et al., 2019; Urry et al., 2021) suggest the modality debate is a red herring. What matters is whether the note-taker engages in generative processing (Wittrock, 1974; Fiorella & Mayer, 2016).

4. **Anchoring benefits are undertheorized**: There is strong practitioner/system evidence that annotations linked to source passages are valuable (Marshall, 1997; Kalir et al., 2020; List & Lin, 2023), but no clean experimental isolation of *anchored vs. unanchored* notes holding other factors constant. This is a gap.

5. **Temporal dynamics are almost entirely unstudied**: No empirical work directly examines how note content/quality evolves *within* a single reading session, or how the timing of notes relative to reading affects learning. Forte's progressive summarization is a practitioner framework, not an empirical finding. This is a significant gap worth pursuing.

6. **Eye-tracking + note-taking integration is nascent**: PeyeDF (Filetti et al., 2019) is the only system paper combining these. Zhang et al. (2025) used eye-tracking to study annotation quality but did not close the loop (using gaze to adapt annotation scaffolding). Shubi et al. (2024) showed reading goals are decodable from gaze. The pieces exist but have not been assembled.

### What This Means for System Design

A system that captures reading context + notes should:
- Scaffold generative processing (paraphrasing prompts, summarization templates) rather than encouraging verbatim copying
- Anchor notes to specific passages in the source text
- Minimize UI friction to preserve cognitive resources for germane processing
- Support both encoding (active annotation) and storage (searchable, reviewable notes linked to source)
- Consider hybrid input (typed for speed, pen for depth)
- Potentially use reading behavior signals (time on passage, regressions) to identify moments where annotation would be most valuable
